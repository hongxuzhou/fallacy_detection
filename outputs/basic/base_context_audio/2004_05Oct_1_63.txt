False Cause (3) is about mistaking correlation for causation. The speaker is saying that because Saddam is no longer in power (cause), the attacks decreased (effect). But is there a direct causal link? Maybe there's another factor, like other policies or changes in the region. The speaker is assuming that Saddam's removal is the reason, which might not be the case. That's a classic False Cause fallacy. 

Alternatively, could it be something else? Like Appeal to Authority? No, because the speaker isn't citing an authority. Ad Hominem? No, they're not attacking the person. Appeal to Emotion? The statement is more about cause than emotion. Slippery Slope? Not really, because it's not predicting a chain of events. Slogans? The statement isn't a slogan but a claim. 

So the most likely is False Cause (3). The speaker is implying that Saddam's absence caused the decrease in attacks, without sufficient evidence that there's a direct causal relationship.
