The statement says that if the federal government gets the power to pay teachers, it will inevitably gain the power to set standards and tell them what to teach, and that this would be bad for the country and the teaching profession.

First, I need to break down the argument. The speaker is making a claim that a certain action (government paying teachers) will lead to a series of negative consequences (setting standards, controlling teaching content). The key here is the causal link between the initial action and the subsequent outcomes. 

Looking at the fallacy categories provided, the main one that comes to mind is False Cause (3). False Cause is when there's a misinterpretation of correlation as causation. The speaker is assuming that because the government has the power to pay teachers, it will automatically gain the power to set standards and control teaching. There's a leap here from having payment authority to having control over curriculum, which might not be a direct or necessary consequence. 

Wait, but could it be a Slippery Slope (4)? Slippery Slope involves an exaggerated outcome from a small initial action. The statement does mention that the outcome would be bad, but the key difference is that Slippery Slope usually involves a chain of events leading to an extreme result, often omitting intermediate steps. Here, the argument is more about a direct causal link rather than a series of steps. The speaker isn't necessarily saying that each step leads to the next in a chain, but rather that the initial action leads directly to the negative outcome. 

Alternatively, maybe it's an Appeal to Emotion (0)? The speaker mentions that it would be bad for the country and the teaching profession, which could be emotional language. However, the main argument is about the causal relationship, not just emotional appeal. The emotional part is the conclusion, not the reasoning. 

Another possibility is Slogans (5), but the statement isn't a catchy phrase or repetition. It's a more direct argument. 

Ad Hominem (2) doesn't seem to fit here. There's no attack on the person's character or their position. 

Appeal to Authority (1) is also not present. The speaker isn't citing an authority figure. 

So, the main fallacy here is False Cause (3) because the speaker is assuming a causal relationship between the government's payment authority and the subsequent control over teaching standards without providing evidence for that link. It's a direct causal claim that might not be justified, hence a False Cause fallacy.
